Early recovery is loud. Your thoughts race. Your emotions swing. Your brain won’t shut up now that the chemicals are gone.

After The Noise is a beginner-friendly guide to early recovery written by someone who’s lived it — not a guru, not a clinician, just someone in recovery who’s lost more than he’s gained and had to learn, the hard way, what actually helps and what’s just noise pretending to be wisdom.

Blending straight talk, humor, Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), SMART Recovery principles, and grounded philosophy, this book is meant to sit beside you in the early days — when clarity feels distant and everything feels personal.

Book Two — Idiolocator

I’ve been through this shit, and I’m still in it.
If you’re reading this, you’re not weak — you’re tired of pretending what you’ve been doing was working.
Open a chapter if you want.

CHAPTER ONE — Don’t Lie to the Map Nobody claps when you decide to stop running. There’s no parade for quitting the thing that was slowly killing you. No trophy for choosing clarity over chaos. Most of the time, the only sound you hear is the static in your own head and the quiet judgment of people who don’t know half the shit you survived. And even the ones who do know? A lot of them still don’t clap. They just watch. Waiting to see if this is real or just another chapter you’ll pretend never happened. That’s life. That’s people. That’s exactly why this decision has to be ten toes. This chapter isn’t about motivation. It’s about orientation. Because before you build anything — a life, a future, a business, an empire, even a peaceful morning — you have to find the Idiolocator. That sign in the mall that says YOU ARE HERE. In recovery, it hits different. It doesn’t say: “You’re doing great.” “You’re almost there.” “You’re special.” It says: You are here. In rehab. Rebuilding. Stripped of shortcuts. No substances. No lies that still work. That dot is humbling as hell. That’s why most people refuse to stand on it. They want to skip ahead. They want to jump straight to the comeback story. They want to talk goals before they admit reality. But the map doesn’t move for fantasies. You don’t get to route yourself out of chaos if you’re lying about where you’re standing. And this is where the Baltimore in me speaks up — not loud, not dramatic, just calm and serious: You can’t fake your location and still make it home. Where I’m from, you learn early: the street doesn’t care what you meant to do. It cares what you did, what you repeated, and whether you respected the situation. Same rule here. So let’s say it clean: You’re not here because you’re weak. You’re here because something finally didn’t work anymore. That’s not failure. That’s information. The Dot Is Sacred Recovery doesn’t start when you stop using. It starts when you stop pretending. CBT calls this reality testing. Hermeticism calls it correspondence — as within, so without. The streets call it keeping it a buck. Different languages. Same law. If you lie about where you are, every move you make after that is crooked. That’s why people relapse with big plans in their mouth. Because the plan might be real — but the starting point is fake. Standing on the dot means: admitting you don’t know everything accepting you’re rebuilding from the ground up letting go of comparisons shutting up long enough to learn That last one is hard. Especially in rehab. Rehab Is the Stoop With Fluorescent Lights Rehab is basically a neighborhood bench with rules. Everybody watching everybody. Everybody clocking who talks smart, who’s performing, who’s really locked in, who’s just killing time. And comparison spreads fast. You see someone reading books, talking strategy, thinking long-term — and something tightens in your chest. That ain’t hate. That’s hunger. But if you don’t check it, jealousy will have you crashing out over timelines you made up in your head. Here’s the Idiolocator theory in real life: Jealousy only hurts when you think you’re supposed to be somewhere else. So you come back to the dot: I’m here. I’m alive. I’m rebuilding. I’m not late. I’m not early. I’m accurate. Accuracy beats hype every time. Don’t Rush the Corner - Baltimore Logic Where I’m from, there’s another rule you learn fast: You don’t rush the corner. You don’t come flying out blind just because you’re emotional, impatient, scared, or feeling yourself. You slow down. You check the angle. You listen before you move. Because rushing the corner gets people hurt. That’s not paranoia. That’s situational awareness. Recovery works the same way. Most relapses happen because people rush the corner. They sprint past the Idiolocator. They skip the dot. They assume they know what’s next. They think: “I’m good now.” “I’ve learned enough.” “I can handle it.” “I’m different.” That’s rushing the corner with your hands in your pockets and your head full of stories. CBT would call it faulty prediction. Hermeticism would call it effect without understanding cause. Baltimore calls it moving reckless. Same mistake. Ten toes doesn’t mean charging forward. Ten toes means planting your feet before you move. You don’t rush the corner with cravings. You don’t rush the corner with money. You don’t rush the corner with relationships. CHAPTER FOUR — The Quiet Signal There’s a point in recovery where something shifts — not loud, not dramatic, not the kind of thing you announce. It’s subtle. The noise doesn’t disappear, but it loses authority. The chaos still shows up, but it doesn’t own the room anymore. And for the first time, you start to feel like you’re not doing this alone — even though nobody just walked in and saved you. That’s where this chapter lives. Not in doctrine. Not in dogma. Not in “you should.” Just in experience. What Spirituality Isn’t Let’s clear the bullshit first. Spirituality isn’t religion unless you want it to be. It’s not church. It’s not slogans. It’s not pretending you’re healed. And it damn sure isn’t bypassing your work by calling it “God’s plan.” If anything, real spirituality makes you more accountable — not less. Early sobriety without spirituality feels like white-knuckling reality. You’re sober, but you’re still alone in your head. You’re not using, but you’re not connected either. That’s just abstinence. Recovery is different. Low Key AA / NA, High Honesty Here’s the truth nobody gets mad at once they’re ready: AA and NA don’t work because of the steps. They don’t work because of the rooms. They don’t work because of the words. They work because people stop trying to do everything alone. That’s it. No mysticism required. You don’t need to agree with every line. You don’t need to adopt anyone’s language. You don’t need to raise your hand and spill your soul on day one. You just need one thing: Willingness to not be the smartest person in the room for once. That’s the real ego death. And that’s where spirituality sneaks in — quietly. Attraction, Not Promotion Nobody ever talked me into believing anything. What got my attention was watching people who were: calm under pressure honest without drama consistent without bragging accountable without shame They weren’t selling anything. They were just different. That’s attraction. You don’t promote recovery. You embody it. You don’t convince people you’ve changed. You let time handle that. You don’t preach spirituality. You move in a way that makes people curious. That’s the signal. To be continued.....

A Quiet Thank You

If my name ends up anywhere near this work, don’t get it twisted:

I don’t do this alone.
I don’t muscle it.
I get carried — and I pay attention.

To my counselors —
You take a mind that’s dangerous to itself and teach it how to check its own paperwork.
CBT shows me how to cross-examine a thought instead of taking the stand for it.
The Hermetic principles give me a compass — as within, so without — fix the inside or keep fighting ghosts outside.
And to the residential staff and peer support who had my back from day one — who showed up daily, handled the unglamorous work, and made sure I didn’t fall through the cracks when nobody was watching.
Your consistency, structure, and willingness to stand in it with me gave me safety before I had stability.
Even the nurses in class, without trying to be anything extra, gave me strength just by showing up steady, competent, and human.
Watching people do their jobs with care reminds me that discipline is survivable — and dignity is learnable.

To my brothers at the center —
Not just IOP, though that’s where most of the bond gets welded.
You don’t save me. You stand next to me while I learn not to quit.
We check each other’s thinking, call out patterns, and take responsibility without turning it into self-hate.
Men sitting honest in a room build order whether they mean to or not.
We prove that daily.

And to a family in North Olmsted —
You saw the mess up close and never flinched.
No lectures. No side-eyes. No moral scoreboard.
You let me sit at the table without earning it first.
You treated relapse like a condition, not a character flaw.
That kind of mercy isn’t loud — it’s disciplined.
Baltimore rule applies: if you don’t kick a man while he’s down, you’re already family.
You never judged me. Ever.
I carry that.

To my sponsor —
Who I don’t call enough.
Who still answers.
You keep reminding me God isn’t impressed by speeches.
He shows up in consistency, restraint, and doing the next right thing — especially when nobody’s watching.
You help me remember I’m not the one running the universe, and that’s a relief.

To God —
Not the billboard version.
The quiet one that doesn’t argue back.
The presence in timing, restraint, and the moment before I do something stupid — if I listen.

To the intelligence — human and artificial —
that helps me organize what’s already there.
You don’t give me answers.
You give me mirrors and better questions.
Tool, not crutch.
Signal, not noise.

And to my son —
You are the reason this work has weight.
You don’t rescue me — you aim me.
Every step I take now asks the same question:
Would this man be safe to follow?
I’m building toward yes.

None of this is dramatic.
It’s repetition.
Accountability.
People who don’t let me lie comfortably.

This isn’t a victory lap.
It’s a location marker.

I’m not here alone.
I stopped fighting the map.

In a universe this loud,
clarity isn’t found —
it’s allowed.

But only after the noise.
